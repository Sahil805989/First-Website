Introduction

Starfish, or sea star, is a common marine animal found from seashore to ocean depths; 1600 species are known worldwide. They belong to the invertebrate phylum echinodermata. Starfish are usually radially symmetrical, often with 5 radiating arms joined to a central disc. Although feeding habits are varied, starfish are often formidable predators which generate frantic escape behaviour in snails and bivalve prey. There are many species on Canada's coasts, ranging in size from tiny (50 mm) Leptasterias hexactis to giant Pycnopodia helianthoides, up to 1 m in diameter and with as many as 24 arms. (Starfish, n.d.)


Habitat

Starfish, often known as sea stars, are intriguing marine animals found in a range of aquatic settings. They can be found in both shallow and deep waters, from the tropics to the poles. Starfish are benthic organisms, meaning they reside on the ocean floor, and they may adapt to a variety of surfaces, including rocks, coral reefs, sandy bottoms, and seagrass beds. Starfish have radial symmetry and normally have five limbs, though some species have more. Their distinct water vascular system aids movement and eating. Starfish are opportunistic feeders, eating a variety of foods such as mollusks, tiny fish, and debris. These hardy organisms are well-adapted to a variety of climatic conditions, including intertidal zones where they may be exposed to air.


(mlblevins, 2011)


Behavior

Starfish, often known as sea stars, exhibit fascinating behaviors in their many surroundings. They travel slowly around surfaces by using tube feet. Their unique feeding method incorporates evertible stomachs, which allow them to ingest and digest meals outside of their bodies. Notably, starfish have the unique capacity to regrow lost arms, which is an important part of their resilience. Some starfish use autotomy to avoid predators by shedding arms as a diversion. They have a basic sense of direction and can tell the difference between light and dark. Skin gills help with respiration and osmoregulation, as well as salt balance. Starfish, which reproduce dioeciously, play an important role in marine ecosystems by managing prey numbers. Their slow movement is in stark contrast to their critical role in nutrient cycle. In a variety of ocean environments, ranging from intertidal zones to deep-sea habitats. (Starfish Behavior - AnimalBehaviorCorner, n.d.)


Media


Starfish (also known as sea stars) are stunning aquatic organisms that come in a range of colors, shapes, and sizes. Although the most common starfish have only five arms, some of these species can develop to have up to 40 arms. The remarkable sea critters, which are classified as echinoderms, navigate using their tube feet. They have remarkable stomachs that allow them to recover damaged limbs and consume huge prey. Sea Stars Are Not Fish:
Although sea stars are usually referred to as "starfish," they are not actual fish. They lack gills, scales, and fins, as do fish. Sea stars move very differently than fish. While fish use their tails to push themselves, sea stars use tiny tube feet to propel themselves. Scientists prefer to refer to starfish as "sea stars" because they are not classified as fish."

Sea Stars Are Echinoderms:
The phylum Echinodermata includes sea stars. They're linked to sand dollars, sea urchins, sea cucumbers, and sea lilies. This phylum has roughly 7,000 species in total. Many echinoderms have radial symmetry, which means that their body components are organized around a central axis. Because their bodies are divided into five pieces, many sea stars feature five-point radial symmetry. This means they don't have a clear left and right half, merely a top and a bottom. Spines are also common in echinoderms, albeit they are less prominent in sea stars than in other species such as sea urchins.

There Are Thousands of Sea Star Species:
There are over 2,000 different types of sea stars. Some reside in the intertidal zone, while others live in deep ocean water. While many species reside in tropical environments, sea stars can also be found in frigid environments, including the polar regions.

Not All Sea Stars Have Five Arms:
While many people are familiar with five-armed sea stars, not all sea stars have only five arms. Some species have many more arms than others, such as the sun star, which can have up to 40. The majority of sea stars' important organs are housed in their arms. This means that some species can regenerate an entire new sea star from a single arm and a part of the central disc. This won't happen overnight; it takes around a year for an arm to regenerate.

Sea Stars Are Protected by Armor:
The skin of a sea star can be leathery or slightly spiky, depending on the species. Sea stars have a strong coating on their upper side comprised of calcium carbonate plates with small spines on their surface. The spines of a sea star protect it from predators such as birds, fish, and sea otters. The crown-of-thorns starfish is a very spiky sea star.

Sea Stars Do Not Have Blood:
Sea stars have a circulatory system that is mostly made up of seawater rather than blood. Through the sieve plate, seawater is pushed into the animal's water circulatory system. This is a trap door known as a madreporite, which is often apparent as a light-colored area on the starfish's top. Seawater moves into the sea star's tube foot from the madreporite, causing the arm to lengthen. To retract the limb, muscles within the tube feet are employed.

(Some Starfish Have 40 Arms, and Other Facts About the Sea Creatures, n.d.)
